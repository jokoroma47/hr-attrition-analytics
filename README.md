# Why Employees Leave — People Analytics & Workforce Attrition

A data analytics project examining the drivers of employee 
attrition across 14,999 employees in 10 departments, 
identifying the specific conditions that make staff most 
likely to leave.

---

## Overview

High attrition is one of the most costly and disruptive 
problems in any organisation. I built this project to 
go beyond the headline number and identify the precise 
combination of factors — workload, satisfaction, salary, 
tenure, promotion history — that predict whether an 
employee will stay or leave.

The dataset covers nearly 15,000 employee records with 
11 attributes per person including satisfaction score, 
performance evaluation, number of projects, monthly hours, 
tenure, salary band, department, work accident history 
and promotion status.

---

## What I Found

- 1 in 4 employees left — an overall attrition rate of 
  23.81% across 14,999 staff
- Satisfaction is the single strongest predictor — leavers 
  average 0.44 vs 0.67 for stayers, a gap of 0.23 points
- Every single employee assigned 7 projects left — 100% 
  attrition with zero exceptions
- Year 5 is the crisis point — 56.6% of employees at the 
  5-year tenure mark leave. Years 7, 8 and 10 show 0%
- Low salary drives 29.7% attrition vs 6.6% for high — 
  a 4.5x multiplier, yet 48.8% of staff are on low salary
- Only 2.13% of employees are promoted in 5 years, yet 
  promoted employees leave at 4x lower rates (6.0% vs 24.2%)
- The HR department has the highest attrition at 29.1% — 
  the team responsible for retention cannot retain itself
- Both under-worked (<150hrs) and overworked (250+hrs) 
  employees leave at over 35% — a clear U-shaped risk curve
- A bimodal evaluation pattern emerges — medium performers 
  (34.2%) and excellent performers (30.8%) both leave at 
  high rates, while only the 0.6-0.8 "good" band is retained
- 1,559 high-risk employees identified — dissatisfied, 
  overworked, and not promoted — with a 60.4% attrition rate
